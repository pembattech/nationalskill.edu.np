About Korea

A Nation of Rich Culture
& Deep History

Korea, a region in East Asia divided into two distinct sovereign states — North Korea and South Korea — boasts a rich and diverse cultural heritage that dates back thousands of years. Despite the division, both share common cultural roots and traditions, while also developing unique characteristics over the decades.

Historical Background

Korean culture has evolved over millennia, with influences from neighboring China and Japan, yet maintaining a distinct identity. The Three Kingdoms period (57 BC – 668 AD), comprising Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla, was pivotal in forming the foundation of Korean culture. The subsequent Goryeo (918–1392) and Joseon (1392–1897) dynasties further shaped Korean culture, introducing Confucianism as a guiding philosophy and witnessing the flourishing of Korean arts and literature.

Language & Literature

The Korean language, Hangul, is a unique writing system created during the Joseon Dynasty in 1443 by King Sejong the Great. Celebrated for its scientific design and ease of learning, Korean literature has a rich history with classical works like "Samguk Yusa" and "The Tale of Chunhyang." Modern Korean literature has gained international acclaim, with authors like Han Kang receiving global recognition.

Arts & Crafts

Korean traditional arts include painting, calligraphy, pottery, and music. The celadon pottery of the Goryeo Dynasty is renowned for its exquisite beauty and craftsmanship. Traditional Korean painting "Minhwa" depicts everyday life with vibrant colors and detailed patterns, while calligraphy holds a significant place in aesthetic expression.

Music & Dance

Traditional Korean music "Gugak" encompasses court music, folk music, and religious music. Instruments like the gayageum (zither-like string instrument) and janggu (hourglass-shaped drum) are integral. Traditional Korean dance such as Seungmu (Buddhist monk dance) and Talchum (mask dance) is characterized by graceful movements and colorful costumes.

Religion & Philosophy

Confucianism, Buddhism, and Shamanism have profoundly influenced Korean culture. Confucianism shaped Korean social structure, ethics, and educational systems. Buddhism continues as a significant spiritual practice, while Shamanism persists in rituals and folk practices, reflecting a deep connection with nature and ancestral spirits.

Cuisine

Korean cuisine "Hansik" is known for its bold flavors and diverse dishes. Kimchi — a fermented vegetable dish — is a staple in Korean meals. Other popular dishes include Bulgogi (marinated beef), Bibimbap (mixed rice with vegetables), and Japchae (stir-fried glass noodles). Korean cuisine emphasizes balance and harmony, featuring a variety of side dishes (banchan) that complement the main course.

Festivals & Holidays

Korean festivals such as Seollal (Lunar New Year) and Chuseok (Harvest Festival) are celebrated with traditional rituals, family gatherings, and special foods. These festivals reflect Korea's agrarian past and its emphasis on family and ancestral respect.

Modern Culture — Hallyu 한류

South Korea has become a global cultural powerhouse through the Korean Wave — encompassing K-pop, K-dramas, and films. Internationally acclaimed groups like BTS and Blackpink, along with award-winning films like "Parasite," have significantly boosted Korea's cultural influence worldwide. South Korea's rise as a technology and cultural hub makes Korean language skills increasingly valuable.
